Since 1950, the global child mortality rate is going down and the numbers are predicted to go even lower in the later years. We have come a long way since then, mainly due to efforts from skilled individuals; more accessible nutrition; increased knowledge and awareness; and overall improvement of water, sanitation and hygiene.

However, there is still much to be done in regards to another kind of epidemic in child mortality, that is road-traffic accidents leading to fatal injuries.

In 2017, there was a total of 49,068 under 5 deaths by road-traffic accidents worldwide.
(IHME, Global Burden of Disease)

The Future

For these numbers to go down, drastic changes need to be made which is going to take a lot of time and effort from all us.

But by collaborating and supporting each other all over the world as we do our part, we can as early as 2027 significantly reduce the number of children dying from fatal injuries like road-traffic accidents.

Being Road Safe

So, what can you do to prevent such devastating incidents from happening?

Why are they vulnerable?

  • They don't wear restraints or wear it incorrectly.
  • Are not seen by drivers when entering or exiting cars due to their smaller stature.
  • May distract the driver or engage in some other inappropriate behaviour while driving.
  • Don't realise or think about the consequences of their actions.

Boys are 3 times as more likely to be killed in a road traffic accident than girls.
(World Health Organisation, 2020)

Tips and Strategies

Though there is no way to predict an incoming accident, there are rules you can teach your children about road safety and what you can do to help avoid it. According to Better Health Channel you can:

Talk about the traffic environment.

Hold child’s hand when near cars and explain its importance.

Explain and get them involved in crossing safely, but you will have the final say.

Be a good role mode by wearing a seatbelt, obeying road rules, driving courteously and crossing roads.

Make them have eye contact with road users, especially at intersections.

Involve child in choosing safe places to play.

Separate play areas from driveways.

Ask if child’s early childhood service program includes road safety education.

These are just some of the basic rules you can teach a child.
